👮 The Bayesian Brain

Why Probability over Population Vectors?

A Population Vector is a "blind" machine. Like a cricket's escape trajectory, it just adds up neuron firing rate "arrows" and spits out a single directional number. But what if it is dark and foggy? A population vector cannot tell you if it is confident or guessing.

By using Probability Curves, the brain can mathematically calculate Uncertainty. A wide, flat curve means "I have no idea what is going on." A sharp, thin curve means "I am 100% sure." This allows the brain to weigh its raw sensory evidence (Likelihood) against its formal past experience (Priors) to survive.

👮 The 3 Detectives (Broken Speedometer)

1. The Evidence (Likelihood)
A blindfolded cop has a broken radar gun. It reads a speed, but it has random static. It outputs a blurry, wide probability curve.
2. The Context (Prior)
The cop knows their location: a Complex City where cars are usually either slow Locals (30 mph) or fast Speeders (75 mph).
3. The Final Calculation (Posterior)
The brain mathematically multiplies the Radar (Blue) by the Memory (Green) to form the Final Belief (Red). But how does it pick just ONE single number to write on the speeding ticket?

Strategy 1: Maximum Likelihood (ML)

This detective is arrogant and ignores all context. They look purely at the raw sensory evidence (the radar) and pick the absolute highest peak. They naively believe that the broken radar gun is perfectly accurate at that one specific point, completely ignoring the fact that they are standing in a complex city.

Strategies 2 & 3: MAP vs. Bayes (The Smart Ones)

Both of these smart detectives combine the Radar and the Prior Memory to generate a final Probability Curve of possible speeds. Use the dropdown below to change the environment and see how the two strategies split when uncertainty is high.

Strategy 2: MAP Estimator

Maximum A Posteriori. This detective picks the absolute highest peak of the final curve. This is often correct, but it makes massive errors if the peak is wrong.

Strategy 3: Bayes Estimator

Instead of picking the peak, this cautious detective calculates the mathematical Center of Mass (Average) of the whole curve. Their goal is to guarantee they never make a catastrophic error.

👮 Minimizing the Penalty (Why Bayes Beats MAP)

Select "Complex City" in the graph above. The MAP Detective arrogantly picks the top peak. The Bayes Detective looks at the whole curve and calculates the center of mass. Why?

The Logic of minimizing Loss:
By picking the peak, MAP minimizes the number of errors. But if a car is actually a speeder (going 75 mph), MAP's error is catastrophic. The penalty squares the error (75 - 31 = 44² = 1,936 penalty points!).

By guessing a speed in the middle, the Bayes Estimator minimizes the squared error. It ensures that even when it is wrong, it is never disastrously wrong. This is the optimal mathematical strategy for cutting expected loss in an uncertain environment.
